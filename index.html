Module 1

The Big Mindset Shift

From Loan Sales Officer to Portfolio Builder

⏱ 20 minutes 📝 5 Knowledge Check Questions
💭

Picture This...

₦5,000,000

You have just disbursed a ₦5,000,000 loan to a merchant.

Have you successfully completed your job?
⚡

Here's the Truth

Loan Disbursement ≠ Success

Loan Repayment = Success

Disbursing a loan is just the first step. Your job is only truly complete when the merchant has fully repaid. Until then, the work continues.

Let's Think Deeper

🤔 When is your job truly complete as a Loan Sales Officer?
Your job is complete only when the loan is fully repaid with all interest. Disbursement is the beginning, not the end. Every loan you sell becomes part of your portfolio, and the health of that portfolio reflects your judgement.
🤔 If the merchant never repays, was it a successful sale?
Absolutely not. A sale that results in a default is not a success—it's a liability. It hurts the business, wastes resources, and damages the merchant's credit. True success is measured by repayment, not disbursement.
🤔 What does a portfolio with 70% defaults say about your judgement?
A 70% default rate indicates poor merchant selection. It suggests that the Loan Sales Officer prioritized quantity over quality, failed to properly assess merchant viability, and did not apply the SAFE framework. Your portfolio is a reflection of your decision-making.
🤔 Who contributes more to the business?
₦100M Disbursed
10% Repayment
= ₦10M recovered
VS
₦10M Disbursed
90% Repayment
= ₦9M recovered

The second officer demonstrates better judgement and contributes nearly as much to the bottom line with far less risk. Smart Lending is about quality, not just volume.

Traditional Sales Thinking vs Smart Lending Thinking

❌

Traditional Sales Thinking

  • Focus on disbursement volume
  • Success = hitting disbursement targets
  • Merchant is a customer number
  • Move on after disbursement
  • Quantity over quality
  • Short-term sales mindset
  • Collections is "someone else's problem"
  • Celebrate disbursement day
✅

Smart Lending Thinking

  • Focus on portfolio health
  • Success = repayment rates
  • Merchant is a business partner
  • Support merchant through repayment
  • Quality over quantity
  • Long-term portfolio building
  • Collections starts at origination
  • Celebrate full repayment day
👑

The Golden Rule of Smart Lending

"Never recommend a merchant simply because they qualify for a loan. Recommend them because you are confident they can repay it."

Identifying Quality Merchants

SAFE Framework & Merchant Quality Scorecard

⏱ 35 minutes 📝 10 Knowledge Check Questions

The SAFE Framework

A comprehensive approach to merchant evaluation

S

Stability

How long has the business been operating?

Meaning

Stability refers to how long the merchant's business has been operational and how consistent their revenue streams are. A stable business has demonstrated its ability to survive market fluctuations and maintain operations over time.

What to Observe

  • Years in operation (minimum 1-2 years preferred)
  • Consistency of business location
  • Revenue patterns (seasonal vs steady)
  • Number of employees and staff retention
  • Business registration and licenses

Questions to Ask

  • How long have you been running this business?
  • Have you operated from the same location throughout?
  • What are your busiest and slowest months?
  • How many staff do you employ? How long have they worked for you?
  • Do you have a business registration certificate?

Real-life Examples

Good: A grocery store that has been operating in the same location for 5 years, with consistent monthly revenue of ₦2-3M and 4 employees who have been with the business for 2+ years.

Risky: A new boutique that opened 3 months ago with no established customer base and fluctuating revenue.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing business age with business health
  • Not verifying the actual operational history
  • Ignoring seasonal revenue fluctuations

Best Practices

  • Always verify operational history through physical visits
  • Check for consistent revenue over at least 6-12 months
  • Assess the business's ability to withstand economic shocks
A

Ability to Repay

Can the business generate enough cash flow?

Meaning

Ability to Repay measures whether the merchant's business generates sufficient and reliable cash flow to meet loan repayment obligations without straining operations.

What to Observe

  • Daily/weekly/monthly revenue
  • Profit margins (gross and net)
  • Operating expenses and overhead
  • Existing debt obligations
  • Cash flow patterns and cycles

Questions to Ask

  • What is your average daily or weekly revenue?
  • What are your major expenses each month?
  • Do you have any other loans or obligations?
  • How much profit do you take home monthly?
  • What would happen if your revenue dropped by 30%?

Real-life Examples

Good: A restaurant with daily revenue of ₦80,000, monthly expenses of ₦1.2M, net profit of ₦1.2M/month, requesting a ₦5,000,000 loan with monthly repayment of ₦500,000.

Risky: A fashion business with irregular weekly revenue, thin margins, and already servicing two other loans.

Common Mistakes

  • Relying solely on stated revenue without verification
  • Not considering existing debt service obligations
  • Ignoring personal expenses that may affect business cash flow

Best Practices

  • Triangulate revenue data from multiple sources (bank statements, stock records, customer traffic)
  • Ensure Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) is at least 1.5x
  • Verify expenses through supplier receipts and rent agreements
F

Financial Discipline

Does the merchant manage money responsibly?

Meaning

Financial Discipline assesses how the merchant manages their finances, including record-keeping, separation of business and personal funds, savings habits, and past credit behavior.

What to Observe

  • Quality of financial records
  • Separation of business and personal accounts
  • Savings history and patterns
  • Past loan repayment history
  • Inventory management

Questions to Ask

  • Do you keep records of your daily sales?
  • Do you have a separate bank account for your business?
  • How much do you save monthly?
  • Have you taken a loan before? How did you repay it?
  • How do you track your inventory?

Real-life Examples

Good: A merchant who maintains a separate business account, keeps daily sales records in a notebook or app, saves ₦50,000 monthly, and has a clean repayment history on a previous loan.

Risky: A merchant with no records, mixes business and personal funds, has no savings, and has missed payments on previous obligations.

Common Mistakes

  • Accepting verbal claims without documentation
  • Not checking credit bureau reports
  • Overlooking informal savings patterns

Best Practices

  • Request bank statements for the last 3-6 months
  • Check credit bureau history
  • Observe the merchant's physical shop organization as a proxy for financial organization
E

Engagement

Is the merchant committed and responsive?

Meaning

Engagement measures the merchant's willingness to cooperate, transparency in sharing information, responsiveness to communication, and commitment to the loan process.

What to Observe

  • Responsiveness to calls and messages
  • Willingness to provide documentation
  • Transparency about business challenges
  • Attitude during the application process
  • References and community reputation

Questions to Ask

  • How quickly do you respond to customer inquiries?
  • Can you provide references from suppliers or customers?
  • What challenges is your business currently facing?
  • How do you handle customer complaints?
  • Are you willing to sign up for automatic repayment?

Real-life Examples

Good: A merchant who promptly provides all required documents, answers calls, is transparent about a temporary business slowdown, and has good relationships with suppliers.

Risky: A merchant who is evasive about providing documents, doesn't answer calls, and seems to be hiding information about their business.

Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring red flags in merchant behavior
  • Not verifying references
  • Assuming responsiveness during application will continue after disbursement

Best Practices

  • Build rapport to encourage transparency
  • Check references and community standing
  • Set clear expectations about communication from the start

The Loan Has Been Disbursed... Now What?

90-Day Merchant Success Journey & Post-Disbursement Management

⏱ 30 minutes 📝 8 Knowledge Check Questions
✅

The merchant has collected the loan.

Has your work been completed?
💡

Remember:

Repayment is Success

Disbursement is the beginning of your real work. Now you must guide the merchant through the 90-Day Success Journey to ensure full repayment.

The 90-Day Merchant Success Journey

Day 1

Disbursement Day

Objectives: Ensure the merchant receives the funds and understands the terms

BD Activities: Confirm fund receipt, review repayment schedule, answer questions

Expected Outcomes: Merchant is clear on terms and repayment dates

Warning Signs: Merchant seems confused or requests changes to terms

3-5 Days

First Check-In

Objectives: Verify funds are being used as intended

BD Activities: Visit merchant, observe business activity, document fund usage

Expected Outcomes: Funds are being deployed for stated business purpose

Warning Signs: Funds not used as agreed, business seems unchanged

1-2 Weeks

Business Activity Check

Objectives: Monitor business performance and early impact of loan

BD Activities: Review stock levels, customer traffic, revenue trends

Expected Outcomes: Visible improvement in stock or business activity

Warning Signs: No visible change or business seems slower than usual

2 Weeks Before Due

Pre-Repayment Preparation

Objectives: Prepare merchant for upcoming repayment

BD Activities: Remind merchant of due date, confirm amount, discuss any challenges

Expected Outcomes: Merchant acknowledges and is prepared for repayment

Warning Signs: Merchant avoids conversation, makes excuses, or seems stressed

1 Week Before Due

Final Reminder

Objectives: Ensure repayment readiness

BD Activities: Confirm payment method, address any last-minute concerns, reinforce commitment

Expected Outcomes: Merchant confirms readiness to repay

Warning Signs: Merchant lacks funds, asks for extension, or is unreachable

Due Date

Repayment Day

Objectives: Successful repayment collection

BD Activities: Confirm payment is made, thank merchant, document successful repayment

Expected Outcomes: Full repayment received

Warning Signs: Payment not received, merchant unreachable

3 Days After Due

Early Delinquency Check

Objectives: Address any delay immediately

BD Activities: Immediate contact, understand reason, create repayment plan

Expected Outcomes: Merchant commits to new payment date

Warning Signs: No response, broken promises, avoidance

7-14 Days After Due

Escalation Point

Objectives: Escalate to collections team

BD Activities: Handover with complete documentation, provide context

Expected Outcomes: Smooth transition to collections

Warning Signs: Merchant disappears, business closed, phone off

Traffic Light Monitoring System

Green Merchant

Merchant Behaviour: Pays on time, communicates proactively, business is thriving

Business Health: Strong revenue, good cash flow, stable operations

Risk Level: Low

Required Action: Maintain relationship, consider graduation to higher loan amounts

Escalation Process: No escalation needed

Best Practices for Green Merchants

  • Send thank-you messages after repayment
  • Maintain regular check-ins (monthly)
  • Offer loyalty benefits or faster processing for future loans
  • Request referrals to other quality merchants

Yellow Merchant

Merchant Behaviour: Sometimes late, occasionally unreachable, business showing some stress

Business Health: Revenue fluctuations, some operational challenges

Risk Level: Medium

Required Action: Increase monitoring frequency, understand challenges, provide support

Escalation Process: Flag to team lead, create intervention plan

Best Practices for Yellow Merchants

  • Increase check-in frequency to weekly
  • Understand root cause of business challenges
  • Offer business advice or connect to resources
  • Consider restructuring if needed

Red Merchant

Merchant Behaviour: Consistent delinquency, unreachable, business may be distressed or closed

Business Health: Severe revenue decline or business closure

Risk Level: High

Required Action: Immediate escalation to collections, prepare legal action if needed

Escalation Process: Full handover to collections team with all documentation

Best Practices for Red Merchants

  • Document all communication attempts
  • Escalate early rather than waiting
  • Provide complete merchant file to collections
  • Analyze what went wrong to improve future selection
